Everest

Mount Everest or Éverest is the highest mountain on the surface of planet Earth, note 1 with an altitude of 8,848.86 meters (29,032 feet) above sea level. 1 It is located on the Asian continent, in the Himalayan mountain range, specifically in the Mahalangur Himal submountain range; It marks the border between China and Nepal, considered the highest border in the world.4 The massif includes the neighboring peaks Lhotse, 8,516 meters (27,940 ft); Nuptse, 7,855 m (25,771 ft) and Changtse, 7,580 m (24,869 ft).

Information

Program 1

The climbing season on Mount Everest typically occurs during May, when weather conditions are most favorable. During this time, temperatures can range from -60°C (-76°F) at night to 0°C (32°F) during the day. The mountain experiences severe weather conditions, including hurricane-force winds and sudden storms.

Program 2

The journey to Everest's summit typically takes about two months in total. This includes establishing various camps along the route and allowing time for acclimatization. Climbers must pass through the infamous Khumbu Icefall, considered one of the most dangerous stages of the climb due to its unstable nature.

Program 3

The first successful ascent of Mount Everest was achieved on May 29, 1953, by Sir Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. Since then, the mountain has seen over 4,000 successful climbs. Despite modern technology and equipment, Everest remains one of the world's most challenging and dangerous climbs, with over 300 people having lost their lives on its slopes since the first recorded deaths in 1922.

Program 4

Mount Everest's ecosystem is unique and fragile. The mountain hosts several species adapted to extreme conditions, including the Himalayan tahr, snow leopard, and various bird species that can fly at high altitudes. The plant life is equally remarkable, with species like the Himalayan blue poppy found at elevations up to 6,000 meters (19,685 feet).

Program 5

The base camps of Everest serve as crucial staging points for climbers. The South Base Camp in Nepal (5,364m/17,598ft) and North Base Camp in Tibet (5,150m/16,900ft) are small communities during climbing season, complete with medical facilities, communication systems, and support staff. These camps have become legendary in mountaineering culture.

Program 6

Modern climbing on Everest faces new challenges and controversies. The commercialization of expeditions, overcrowding on popular routes, and the impact of climate change are major concerns. The melting of glaciers is exposing frozen bodies and decades of accumulated waste, while also creating new hazards for climbers through unstable ice formations.
